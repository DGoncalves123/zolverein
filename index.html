1834
Lobbying State

Friedrich von Motz and Rhenish merchants establish the Deutscher Zollverein customs union

Prussian Finance Minister Friedrich von Motz, working with Rhenish manufacturers and merchants, organized the Zollverein customs union. These industrialists sought to eliminate internal tariffs in order to gain direct access to larger markets for their goods while excluding Austrian and British products from competing on equal terms.

Source: "The Common Market and the Zollverein: Experiences in Integration," Cambridge Core
1847
Haniel / Zollverein

Franz Haniel sinks the first shaft of the Zollverein mine

Franz Haniel, a Duisburg industrialist, sank the Zollverein shaft in Katernberg after test drilling revealed a thick seam of high-quality "fat coal" suitable for coke production. Haniel required coke for his own steel production. He structured ownership as a Gewerkschaft (mining corporation), distributing shares exclusively among his family members and the landowner of the territory, thereby ensuring all profits remained within a closed circle.

Haniel named the mine after the Zollverein customs union, directly associating his enterprise with the market access the union provided.

Sources: Wikipedia – Zollverein Coal Mine; World Heritage Memory Net; Lokalkompass – "Haniel und Krupp auf Zollverein"; Google Arts & Culture – "A Journey Into History"
1851
Krupp State

Alfred Krupp displays a cast-steel six-pounder cannon at the London World's Fair

Alfred Krupp traveled to London and exhibited a flawless cast-steel cannon. His purpose was to convince Prussian military officials that steel weapons were superior to bronze, thereby creating a new, state-funded market for his products.

Sources: publishing.cdlib.org – "The Origins and Announcement of the Competition"; GlobalSecurity.org – "Krupp"
1859
Krupp State

Prince Regent Wilhelm places an order for 300 steel cannons from Krupp

The Prince Regent of Prussia, later Emperor Wilhelm I, ordered 300 steel cannons from Krupp. This order resulted from Krupp's persistent direct lobbying of the monarchy and military. The order provided Krupp with the capital to build his first complete gun-manufacturing shop in 1861.

Sources: GlobalSecurity.org – "Krupp"; thyssenkrupp.com – company history; yumpu.com – "72 MERCHANTS OF DEATH"
1865
Banking State

Gerson von Bleichröder engineers the privatization of the Cologne-Minden railway

Gerson von Bleichröder, Bismarck's personal banker, proposed that the Prussian government renounce its ownership stake and future rights to the profitable Cologne-Minden railway in exchange for immediate cash. Bleichröder was a director of the railway company himself, meaning the transaction directly enriched him as a major shareholder.

To execute the transaction, he formed a purchasing syndicate with the Cologne-based Oppenheim family and the Rothschild banking family, ensuring profits went directly to his own business circle. The government received 13 million thalers in cash, and 14 million thalers of previously locked government-guaranteed capital was freed for spending.

Sources: Economic Reference News – "Privatization of the Cologne-Minden Railway"; Jewish Encyclopedia – "BLEICHRÖDER, GERSON, BARON VON"
1866
Krupp Banking State

The railway privatization cash pays Krupp for Austro-Prussian War artillery

The funds extracted by Bleichröder from the Cologne-Minden railway privatization became the direct source of payment for Krupp's military contracts. The cash financed the "military total mobilization" and "supported the next 7 weeks of war" against Austria.

During these exact weeks, Krupp's cannons saw their first major combat test. Bleichröder's scheme provided the liquidity that enabled payment to Krupp for mass-producing the weaponry Krupp had long lobbied the state to adopt. The transaction formed a closed loop: Bleichröder and his banking syndicate extracted millions from a state asset, and those same millions were immediately funneled back to Krupp, another key figure in Bismarck's industrial network.

Sources: Economic Reference News – "The Privatization of the Cologne-Minden Railway"; GlobalSecurity.org – "Krupp – 1826-1887"
1870
Krupp State

Krupp's cannons are used in the Franco-Prussian War

The Franco-Prussian War served as a direct demonstration of Krupp's products. The Prussian victory generated massive French war reparations, which fueled a speculative investment boom in the Ruhr. Much of this capital flowed into mining and heavy industry, further enriching the families who owned those enterprises.

Sources: GlobalSecurity.org – "Krupp"; Wikipedia – "History of the Ruhr"
1871
Lobbying

William Thomas Mulvany and Rhenish-Westphalian industrialists found the Langnam-Verein

On March 30, 1871, on the initiative of William Thomas Mulvany, Rhenish-Westphalian iron, textile, and coal industrialists founded the Langnam-Verein. The organization's explicit purpose was to lobby the new German Reich government for protective tariffs and legal tolerance of cartels—policies that directly benefited the founding industrialists' own enterprises.

Source: de-academic.com – "Langnam-Verein"
1873
Krupp

Alfred Krupp completes Villa Hügel with a study above the horse stables

Alfred Krupp completed his 269-room Villa Hügel. He situated his private study directly above the horse stables because he believed the smell of manure benefited his health.

Source: travelinos.com – "Villa Hügel in Essen"
1873
Krupp Banking State

Alfred Krupp is bailed out by a bank consortium arranged by the Prussian State Bank

Krupp's overleveraged expansion nearly caused his bankruptcy. He was saved by a 30-million-mark loan arranged by a consortium of banks at the direction of the Prussian State Bank. This bailout demonstrated that Krupp's creditors and the state would absorb his private losses rather than allow a key military supplier to fail.

Source: encyclopedia2.thefreedictionary.com – "Krupp"
1876
Lobbying

Wilhelm von Kardorff founds the Centralverband Deutscher Industrieller

Reichstag deputy Wilhelm von Kardorff founded the Centralverband Deutscher Industrieller on February 15, 1876. This national-level lobby group successfully influenced a Reichstag commission to recommend interventionist policies that protected heavy industry's cartels and high tariffs.

Sources: Wikipedia – "Centralverband Deutscher Industrieller"; de-academic.com – "Centralverband Deutscher Industrieller"
1893
Lobbying

Emil Kirdorf founds the Rhenish-Westphalian Coal Syndicate

Emil Kirdorf founded the Rhenish-Westphalian Coal Syndicate (RWKS) in February 1893. The cartel gave Kirdorf and a small group of other mine owners direct control over coal prices and production levels in the Ruhr, eliminating price competition among themselves.

Sources: Wikipedia – "Rhenish-Westphalian Coal Syndicate"; Wikipedia – "Emil Kirdorf"
1897
Lobbying State

The Reichsgericht rules that cartel agreements are legally enforceable contracts

On February 4, 1897, Germany's highest court, the Reichsgericht, ruled that cartel agreements were legally enforceable contracts and that violators could be taken to court. This decision was the culmination of decades of lobbying by heavy industrialists—through organizations like the Langnam-Verein and the Centralverband—to create a legal environment that explicitly permitted monopolies.

Source: H-Net – "Spencer on Richter"
1920
Haniel / Zollverein Thyssen

The Haniel family brings Zollverein into a syndicate with the Phönix group (Thyssen empire)

The Haniel family, who had owned Zollverein for 73 years since Franz Haniel sank the first shaft, formed a syndicate with Phönix AG, a mining company that was part of August Thyssen's industrial empire. The transaction integrated Zollverein's coal production directly into Thyssen's steel mills, securing a captive market for the mine and a guaranteed fuel supply for the mills.

Source: Wikipedia – "Zollverein Coal Mine Industrial Complex"
1928
Krupp Lobbying

Paul Reusch convenes the first secret meeting of the Ruhrlade at Villa Hügel

On the evening of January 9, 1928, Paul Reusch, managing director of Gutehoffnungshütte, gathered twelve of the most prominent German heavy industrialists, attired in tuxedos, at the Krupp family's Villa Hügel. This first meeting of the Ruhrlade established a secret organization for coordinating political donations and strategy to shape Weimar Republic policies in favor of heavy industry.

Source: Cambridge Core – "The Ruhrlade"
1950
European Integration Lobbying

Jean Monnet and Robert Schuman propose the European Coal and Steel Community

Former investment banker Jean Monnet drafted the Schuman Plan, which French Foreign Minister Robert Schuman announced on May 9, 1950. The plan's direct purpose was to solve a material supply problem: French steelmakers needed cheap Ruhr coal to compete.

As Time magazine reported, "French steel makers believe that cheap German coal brought to the smelters of Lorraine will enable them to produce steel much more competitively." German industrialists supported the plan because it allowed them to avoid the Allies' plans to dismantle their cartels, arguing that "the old cartel arrangements were economical and efficient, that any change would give the French an unfair advantage."

Sources: Time – "ECONOMICS: Coal-Steel Pool"; Time – "ECONOMICS: Schuman Plan Deadlock"
1968
RAG State

19 Ruhr industrial companies consolidate their coal assets into Ruhrkohle AG

On November 27, 1968, 19 companies in the Ruhr area consolidated their coal mining assets into the newly formed Ruhrkohle AG (RAG). The consolidation was forced by the government's threat to withdraw all subsidies.

As the company's own history states, "Ruhrkohle AG never paid any dividends to its shareholders," confirming its function was not to generate profit for owners but to manage the "socially compatible" disposal of unprofitable assets with public funds.

Sources: Wikipedia – "RAG AG"; history.evonik.com – "History of Evonik Industries"; referenceforbusiness.com – "RAG AG Company Profile"
1983
RAG Haniel / Zollverein

Ruhrkohle AG decides to close the Zollverein mines by the end of 1986

Ruhrkohle AG decided to close the Zollverein mines by year-end 1986. The company's stated plan was "den Rückbau und Abriss der Schachtanlage Zollverein XII"—complete demolition and removal of the structures for scrap.

Sources: zollverein.de – "Vom Bergwerk zum UNESCO-Welterbe"; dw.com – "Schicht im Schacht"
1986
RAG Haniel / Zollverein State

North Rhine-Westphalia purchases Zollverein from RAG to prevent demolition

The state of North Rhine-Westphalia placed the Zollverein complex under monument protection on December 16, 1986, and purchased it from RAG to preserve it as an industrial monument rather than allowing the owner to scrap it. The mine ceased operations on December 23, 1986.

Source: zollverein.de – "Vom Bergwerk zum UNESCO-Welterbe"
1993
RAG Haniel / Zollverein

The Zollverein coking plant closes on June 30

Ruhrkohle AG shut down the Zollverein coking plant on June 30, 1993, due to a decline in steel production and a resulting fall in demand for coke. Plans to sell the plant to China fell through.

Sources: archiv.ruhrtriennale.de – "RuhrTriennale 2005-2007"; baukunst-nrw.de – "Kokerei Zollverein"
1999
Krupp Thyssen

The Thyssen and Krupp families merge their industrial empires

On March 17, 1999, Thyssen AG and Krupp merged into ThyssenKrupp AG. The merger's aim was "to solve the cost and production problems facing the iron and steel industry caused by excess capacities."

Sources: Wikipedia – "ThyssenKrupp"; thyssenkrupp.com – "History"
2001
Haniel / Zollverein European

UNESCO inscribes Zollverein as a World Heritage Site

On December 14, 2001, the UNESCO World Heritage Committee inscribed the Zollverein Coal Mine Industrial Complex on the World Heritage List.

Sources: Wikipedia – "Zollverein Coal Mine Industrial Complex"; zollverein.de – "The heritage of mankind"
2007
RAG Krupp Thyssen State Banking

Werner Müller creates the RAG-Stiftung, allowing E.ON, RWE, and ThyssenKrupp to offload perpetual mining liabilities

Werner Müller, former Bundeswirtschaftsminister and then CEO of RAG, founded the RAG-Stiftung on June 26, 2007. He structured a deal whereby the existing RAG shareholders—E.ON AG, RWE AG, ThyssenKrupp AG, and Société Nouvelle Sidéchar SARL—sold their shares to the new foundation for a symbolic one euro each.

In exchange, these corporations permanently shed all liability for perpetual mining damages: groundwater pumping, subsidence repair, and water purification. Müller simultaneously spun off RAG's profitable chemicals division into Evonik Industries AG, with the RAG-Stiftung holding a majority stake (initially 64.3%). Dividends from Evonik now flow to the foundation to pay the perpetual costs, ensuring the former mine owners escape financial responsibility while the public budget remains untouched.

Sources: Wikipedia – "RAG-Stiftung"; Wikipedia – "Werner Müller"; rag-stiftung.de – "RAG-Stiftung Kapitalanlagen"; Wikipedia – "RAG AG"

Glossary

Zollverein (German: "Customs Union")
A coalition of German states formed to manage tariffs and economic policies within their territories. The Deutscher Zollverein (1834) eliminated internal customs barriers while maintaining external tariffs, creating a unified market that favored member-state industrialists. Franz Haniel later named his mine after this union.
Gewerkschaft (German: "Mining Corporation")
A specific legal form of business organization used in German mining. Unlike a joint-stock company, shares (Kuxe) in a Gewerkschaft could be held in a closed circle and were not publicly traded, allowing families like the Haniels to retain complete control over profits and operations.
Thaler (also Taler)
A silver coin and unit of currency used across the German states before the introduction of the Mark in 1871. One thaler was worth approximately 3 marks. The word "dollar" derives from "thaler."
Cartel (German: Kartell)
A formal agreement among competing firms to fix prices, divide markets, or limit production. Unlike in common law countries where such agreements were generally unenforceable, German courts ruled in 1897 that cartel contracts were legally binding, making Germany uniquely hospitable to industrial monopolies.
Langnam-Verein (Langnamverein)
Short for "Verein zur Wahrung der gemeinsamen wirtschaftlichen Interessen in Rheinland und Westfalen" — the Association for the Protection of Common Economic Interests in the Rhineland and Westphalia. Founded 1871 to lobby for protective tariffs and cartel-friendly legislation.
Centralverband Deutscher Industrieller
The Central Association of German Industrialists. A national-level lobby group founded in 1876, it represented heavy industry interests (steel, coal, iron) at the Reichstag level and successfully pushed for protectionist trade policy and legal tolerance of cartels.
RWKS (Rheinisch-Westfälisches Kohlen-Syndikat)
The Rhenish-Westphalian Coal Syndicate, founded 1893. A cartel that gave its members collective control over coal output and pricing in the Ruhr. At its peak, it controlled over 90% of Ruhr coal production.
Villa Hügel
The 269-room private residence of the Krupp family in Essen, completed in 1873. It served as both a home and a venue for political meetings, including the first gathering of the Ruhrlade in 1928. Today it is a museum and cultural center.
Ruhrlade
A secret organization of twelve leading Ruhr industrialists, founded in 1928 at Villa Hügel. Its purpose was to coordinate political donations and lobby strategy across the Weimar Republic. Members included the heads of major steel, coal, and mining companies.
ECSC (European Coal and Steel Community)
The first supranational European institution, proposed in 1950 via the Schuman Plan. It placed French and German coal and steel production under a common authority. Often cited as the precursor to the European Union, its founding was also driven by French industrialists' desire for cheap Ruhr coal and German industrialists' desire to preserve existing cartel structures.
RAG (Ruhrkohle AG)
A holding company formed in 1968 when 19 Ruhr companies consolidated their coal assets under government pressure. RAG never paid dividends; its function was to manage the orderly decline of coal mining with public subsidies. In 2007 it was restructured into the RAG-Stiftung foundation.
Stiftung (German: "Foundation")
A legal entity under German law that holds assets in perpetuity for a defined purpose. The RAG-Stiftung was created specifically to absorb the perpetual environmental liabilities of Ruhr coal mining, funded by dividends from the profitable Evonik Industries spin-off.
