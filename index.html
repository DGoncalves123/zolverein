Glossary

Zollverein (German: "Customs Union")
A coalition of German states formed in 1834 to manage tariffs and economic policies within their territories. It eliminated internal customs barriers while maintaining external tariffs, creating a unified market favoring member-state industrialists. Franz Haniel named his mine after this union.
Gewerkschaft (German: "Mining Corporation")
A specific legal form of business organization used in German mining. Unlike a joint-stock company, shares (Kuxe) in a Gewerkschaft could be held in a closed circle and were not publicly traded, allowing families like the Haniels to retain complete control over profits and operations.
Thaler (also Taler)
A silver coin and unit of currency used across the German states before the introduction of the Mark in 1871. One thaler was worth approximately 3 marks. The word "dollar" derives from "thaler."
Cartel (German: Kartell)
A formal agreement among competing firms to fix prices, divide markets, or limit production. The German Reichsgericht ruled in 1897 that cartel contracts were legally binding, making Germany uniquely hospitable to industrial monopolies. The same cartel structures were defended again when the ECSC was proposed in 1950.
Langnam-Verein
Short for "Verein zur Wahrung der gemeinsamen wirtschaftlichen Interessen in Rheinland und Westfalen." Founded 1871 to lobby for protective tariffs and cartel-friendly legislation for Rhenish-Westphalian heavy industry.
Centralverband Deutscher Industrieller
The Central Association of German Industrialists. A national-level lobby group founded in 1876, it represented heavy industry interests at the Reichstag level and successfully pushed for protectionist trade policy and legal tolerance of cartels.
RWKS (Rheinisch-Westfälisches Kohlen-Syndikat)
The Rhenish-Westphalian Coal Syndicate, founded 1893. A cartel that gave its members collective control over coal output and pricing in the Ruhr. At its peak it controlled over 90% of Ruhr coal production.
Villa Hügel
The 269-room private residence of the Krupp family in Essen, completed in 1873. It served as both a home and a venue for political meetings, including the first gathering of the Ruhrlade in 1928. Today it is a museum and cultural center.
Ruhrlade
A secret organization of twelve leading Ruhr industrialists, founded in 1928 at Villa Hügel. Its purpose was to coordinate political donations and lobby strategy across the Weimar Republic. Members included the heads of major steel, coal, and mining companies.
Dawes Plan (1924)
An American-designed restructuring of Germany's reparations obligations, largely organized by J.P. Morgan. It placed Germany in financial "receivership," provided an 800-million gold mark loan, and created a circular flow of capital: U.S. loans → German reparations payments → Allied war debt repayments to U.S. banks. Replaced by the Young Plan in 1929. The capital it generated directly financed the Vereinigte Stahlwerke and Zollverein Shaft XII.
UBC (Union Banking Corporation)
A New York bank established in 1924 by Prescott Bush and the Harriman brothers as the American investment arm of Fritz Thyssen's Dutch bank (Bank voor Handel en Scheepvaart). Seized by the U.S. government in 1942 under the Trading with the Enemy Act for funding Nazi Germany. No American directors were charged.
BIS (Bank for International Settlements)
An international financial institution established in 1930 under the Young Plan to administer German reparation payments. Run by Thomas McKittrick, a Wall Street banker connected to Allen Dulles. By 1940 it was "effectively controlled by Hitler's regime" and laundered hundreds of millions in looted gold. Five BIS directors were charged at Nuremberg with war crimes.
Vereinigte Stahlwerke (German Steel Trust)
A steel conglomerate created in 1926 by the New York investment bank Dillon, Read & Co. using Dawes Plan capital. It merged the Thyssen, Phoenix, Rheinstal, and Deutsch-Luxemburg steel works, controlling more than 75% of Germany's ore reserves and employing 200,000 workers. Helped fund the Nazi election campaign in 1933.
Operation Paperclip
A secret U.S. intelligence program, 1945–1959, that brought more than 1,600 German scientists, engineers, and technicians to the United States. Many were confirmed former Nazi Party members, including SS and SA members. The program was valued at $10 billion in patents and industrial processes.
ECSC (European Coal and Steel Community)
The first supranational European institution, proposed in 1950 via the Schuman Plan drafted by former Wall Street banker Jean Monnet. German industrialists supported it specifically because it allowed them to preserve existing cartel structures under a supranational umbrella rather than face Allied dismantlement orders.
RAG (Ruhrkohle AG)
A holding company formed in 1968 when 19 Ruhr companies consolidated their coal assets under government pressure. RAG never paid dividends; its function was to manage the orderly decline of coal mining with public subsidies. In 2007 it was restructured into the RAG-Stiftung foundation.
Stiftung (German: "Foundation")
A legal entity under German law that holds assets in perpetuity for a defined purpose. The RAG-Stiftung was created to absorb the perpetual environmental liabilities of Ruhr coal mining, funded by dividends from the profitable Evonik Industries spin-off, allowing former mine owners to escape financial responsibility.
KfW (Kreditanstalt für Wiederaufbau)
"Reconstruction Credit Institute." Established in 1948 by Anglo-American occupying powers, bankrolled by Marshall Plan funds to channel investment into German industry. Washington viewed it as essential to Western Cold War strategy. Still exists today as Germany's state development bank.
