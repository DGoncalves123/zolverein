1834
Lobbying State

Friedrich von Motz and Rhenish merchants establish the Deutscher Zollverein customs union

Prussian Finance Minister Friedrich von Motz, working with Rhenish manufacturers and merchants, organized the Zollverein to eliminate internal tariffs, giving these industrialists direct access to larger markets while excluding Austrian and British goods.

Source: "The Common Market and the Zollverein: Experiences in Integration," Cambridge Core
1847
Haniel / Zollverein

Franz Haniel sinks the first shaft of the Zollverein mine

Franz Haniel sank the shaft in Katernberg after test drilling revealed thick "fat coal" suitable for coke. He needed coke for his own steel production. Haniel structured ownership as a Gewerkschaft, distributing shares exclusively among family members and the landowner, ensuring profits remained within a closed circle. He named the mine after the Zollverein customs union.

Sources: Wikipedia – Zollverein Coal Mine; lokalkompass.de – "Haniel und Krupp auf Zollverein"; Google Arts & Culture – "A Journey Into History"
1851
Krupp State

Alfred Krupp displays a cast-steel cannon at the London World's Fair

Alfred Krupp exhibited a flawless cast-steel six-pounder cannon to convince Prussian military officials that steel weapons were superior to bronze, thereby creating a new state-funded market for his products.

Sources: publishing.cdlib.org – "The Origins and Announcement of the Competition"; GlobalSecurity.org – "Krupp"
1859
Krupp State

Prince Regent Wilhelm orders 300 steel cannons from Krupp

The Prince Regent of Prussia, later Emperor Wilhelm I, ordered 300 steel cannons from Krupp. This resulted from Krupp's persistent direct lobbying of the monarchy and military. The order provided Krupp with capital to build his first complete gun-manufacturing shop in 1861.

Sources: GlobalSecurity.org – "Krupp"; thyssenkrupp.com
1865
Banking State

Gerson von Bleichröder engineers the privatization of the Cologne-Minden railway

Gerson von Bleichröder, Bismarck's personal banker and a director of the railway company, proposed the Prussian government renounce its ownership stake and future rights to the profitable railway for immediate cash. He formed a purchasing syndicate with the Oppenheim family and the Rothschild banking family. The government received 13 million thalers in cash, and 14 million thalers of guaranteed capital was freed.

Sources: 经济参考报 – "科隆-明登铁路私有化：普奥战争的财源"; Jewish Encyclopedia – "BLEICHRÖDER, GERSON, BARON VON"
1866
Krupp Banking State

The railway privatization cash pays Krupp for Austro-Prussian War artillery

The funds extracted by Bleichröder from the Cologne-Minden railway privatization became the direct payment source for Krupp's military contracts. The cash financed the "military total mobilization" and "supported the next 7 weeks of war" against Austria, during which Krupp's cannons saw their first major combat test.

Bleichröder's scheme formed a closed loop: he and his syndicate extracted millions from a state asset, which were immediately funneled to Krupp for mass-producing the weaponry Krupp had long lobbied the state to adopt.

Sources: 经济参考报 – "科隆-明登铁路私有化：普奥战争的财源"; GlobalSecurity.org – "Krupp 1826-1887"
1870
Krupp State

Krupp's cannons are used in the Franco-Prussian War

The war served as a direct demonstration of Krupp's products. The Prussian victory generated massive French war reparations, which fueled a speculative investment boom in the Ruhr. Much of this capital flowed into mining and heavy industry, further enriching the families who owned those enterprises.

Sources: GlobalSecurity.org – "Krupp"; Wikipedia – "History of the Ruhr"
1871
Lobbying

William Thomas Mulvany and Rhenish-Westphalian industrialists found the Langnam-Verein

On March 30, 1871, on William Thomas Mulvany's initiative, iron, textile, and coal industrialists founded the Langnam-Verein to lobby the new German Reich government for protective tariffs and legal tolerance of cartels—policies that directly benefited their own enterprises.

Source: de-academic.com – "Langnam-Verein"
1873
Krupp

Alfred Krupp completes Villa Hügel with a study above the horse stables

Alfred Krupp completed his 269-room mansion and situated his private study directly above the horse stables because he believed the smell of manure benefited his health.

Source: travelinos.com – "Villa Hügel in Essen"
1873
Krupp Banking State

Alfred Krupp is bailed out by a bank consortium arranged by the Prussian State Bank

Krupp's overleveraged expansion nearly caused bankruptcy. He was saved by a 30-million-mark loan arranged by a consortium of banks at the direction of the Prussian State Bank, demonstrating that his creditors and the state would absorb his private losses rather than allow a key military supplier to fail.

Source: encyclopedia2.thefreedictionary.com – "Krupp"
1876
Lobbying

Wilhelm von Kardorff founds the Centralverband Deutscher Industrieller

Reichstag deputy Wilhelm von Kardorff founded the Centralverband Deutscher Industrieller on February 15, 1876. It successfully influenced a Reichstag commission to recommend interventionist policies that protected heavy industry's cartels and high tariffs.

Source: Wikipedia – "Centralverband Deutscher Industrieller"
1893
Lobbying

Emil Kirdorf founds the Rhenish-Westphalian Coal Syndicate

Emil Kirdorf founded the RWKS cartel in February 1893, giving himself and a small group of mine owners direct control over coal prices and production levels in the Ruhr.

Sources: Wikipedia – "Rhenish-Westphalian Coal Syndicate"; Wikipedia – "Emil Kirdorf"
1897
Lobbying State

The Reichsgericht rules that cartel agreements are legally enforceable contracts

On February 4, 1897, Germany's highest court ruled that cartel agreements were legally enforceable and violators could be taken to court. This decision culminated decades of lobbying through organizations like the Langnam-Verein and the Centralverband to create a legal environment explicitly permitting monopolies.

Source: H-Net – "Spencer on Richter"
1919
US Finance International State

John Foster Dulles and Norman Davis draft Article 231 of the Versailles Treaty—the "war guilt" clause

John Foster Dulles, a Wall Street lawyer representing the American delegation, negotiated the German reparations clauses. He and U.S. diplomat Norman Davis wrote Article 231 "without regard to how the Germans would interpret its moral overtones," using it as a legal pretext to extract payments without setting any fixed sum.

Sources: Origins – "The Versailles Treaty Fight"; Rowman & Littlefield – John Foster Dulles: Piety, Pragmatism, and Power
1919
US Finance International

Thomas Lamont acknowledges that reparations caused more trouble than any other treaty point

Thomas Lamont, the banker representing the American Treasury in Paris, stated that "the subject of reparations caused more trouble, contention, hard feeling, and delay at the Paris Peace Conference than any other point of the Treaty." The treaty assigned a Reparations Commission to notify Germany of the final bill by May 1921.

Sources: Wikipedia – "Thomas W. Lamont"; Wikipedia – "Treaty of Versailles"
1919
International

John Maynard Keynes publishes The Economic Consequences of the Peace, demonstrating Germany cannot pay

John Maynard Keynes, a British Treasury official at the conference, calculated that even with restricted consumption Germany could run an annual trade surplus of at most $250 million—over 30 years totaling only $7.5 billion, far below Allied demands. The work was "strongly endorsed" by economists and confirmed the sums were "impossible to meet."

Source: The Nation, March 6, 1920 – "Keynes's 'Economic Consequences of the Peace'"
1920
Haniel / Zollverein Thyssen

The Haniel family brings Zollverein into a syndicate with the Phönix group (Thyssen empire)

After 73 years of family ownership, the Haniels formed a syndicate with Phönix AG, integrating Zollverein's coal production directly into August Thyssen's steel mills, securing a captive market for the mine and guaranteed fuel for the mills.

Source: Wikipedia – "Zollverein Coal Mine Industrial Complex"
1921
International State

The London Schedule of Payments sets reparations at 132 billion gold marks—deliberately too high to repay

The Reparations Commission fixed the total sum at 132 billion gold marks. Reparations were "purposefully set too high for Germany to be able to repay," feeding hyperinflation that pushed Germany toward financial collapse.

Source: Wikipedia – "World War I reparations"
1923
Steel Trust Cartels

Hugo Stinnes expands his empire during hyperinflation

As the German mark collapsed, Hugo Stinnes "gained cheap credit and bought out competitors and paid off loans," acquiring control of 1,535 companies and roughly 20% of German industry by 1924. He consolidated coal mines, steel mills, shipping lines, newspapers, and banks.

Sources: Wikipedia – "Hugo Stinnes"; Time – "German Business Kaiser"
1924
US Finance International

Charles Dawes, appointed by Herbert Hoover, leads a committee of bankers to design the Dawes Plan

Charles Dawes presided over a committee that produced the Dawes Plan, "largely a J.P. Morgan production" and an "Anglo-American sponsored bankers' bail out of Germany." It provided an initial loan of 800 million gold marks, with the first $200 million loan being "the best-guaranteed loan in the history of international finance." The plan placed Germany in "receivership."

Sources: Wikipedia – "The Dawes Plan"; CADTM – "The Marshall Plan and the Debt Agreement on German Debt"
1924
US Finance International

John Foster Dulles, selected by J.P. Morgan, draws up the Dawes Plan

J.P. Morgan selected Dulles to draw up the plan for reshaping Germany's reparations payments. American and British bankers provided loans enabling Germany to expand production and make reparations payments, creating a circular flow of U.S. capital that returned to American banks as interest.

Source: Wikipedia – "John Foster Dulles"
1924
US Finance German Industry

The Dawes Plan injects $2 billion in American loans into Germany over five years, all flowing back to U.S. banks

The German economy "took off like a rocket, fueled by a massive injection of American loans ($2 billion over the next five years) that were used to pay reparations." The arrangement "envassaled German industry to U.S. or at least non-German bankers."

Source: Time – "German Business Kaiser"
1926
US Finance Thyssen / Steel Trust

Dillon, Read & Co. creates the Vereinigte Stahlwerke (German Steel Trust)

Using capital raised through the Dawes Plan, the New York investment bank Dillon, Read & Co. merged the Thyssen, Phoenix, Rheinstal, and Deutsch-Luxemburg steel works. The new trust controlled more than 75% of Germany's ore reserves, employed 200,000 workers, and placed two Dillon Read representatives on its board.

Source: Wikipedia – "Vereinigte Stahlwerke"
1926
Haniel / Zollverein Thyssen / Steel Trust US Finance

The Zollverein mine is transferred to the Vereinigte Stahlwerke

The Zollverein coal mine, previously part of the Phönix group since the 1920 Haniel-Thyssen syndicate, was integrated into the newly formed Vereinigte Stahlwerke, placing one of the Ruhr's most productive mines under direct control of the U.S.-financed steel trust.

Source: Wikipedia – "Zollverein Coal Mine Industrial Complex"
1926
Cartels International

The International Steel Cartel is established

Following the Dawes Plan, the International Steel Cartel was created to sustain prices and divide export quotas among producers in Germany, France, Belgium, and Luxembourg. The cartel represented about two-thirds of the world's steel exports.

Source: Wikipedia – "International Steel Cartel"
1928
Krupp Lobbying

Paul Reusch convenes the first secret meeting of the Ruhrlade at Villa Hügel

On January 9, 1928, Paul Reusch, managing director of Gutehoffnungshütte, gathered twelve of the most prominent German heavy industrialists at Krupp's Villa Hügel. This first meeting of the Ruhrlade established a secret organization for coordinating political donations and strategy to shape Weimar Republic policies.

Source: Cambridge Core – "The Ruhrlade"
1928
Haniel / Zollverein Steel Trust US Finance

The Vereinigte Stahlwerke constructs Zollverein Shaft XII

On behalf of the steel trust, architects Fritz Schupp and Martin Kremmer built Shaft XII, which became the world's most powerful underground mine, producing 12,000 tonnes of coal per day. This expansion was directly financed by the capital consolidation achieved through the Dawes Plan and the creation of the steel trust.

Source: Wikipedia – "Zollverein Coal Mine Industrial Complex"
1929
US Finance International

Owen D. Young, a Morgan agent, designs the Young Plan to replace the Dawes Plan

Owen D. Young led a committee that produced the Young Plan in 1929. The plan reduced Germany's total debt by 75% to 121 billion Reichsmarks, payable in 59 annual installments, but required "payments in goods produced in Germany financed by foreign loans."

Sources: Wikipedia – "Young Plan"; Time – "German Business Kaiser"
1930
US Finance International Banking

The Bank for International Settlements (BIS) is created

The Young Plan established the BIS to administer reparation payments and serve as a forum for central bank cooperation. The institution was controlled by the same bankers who drafted the Young Plan, institutionalizing the flow of capital and debt underpinning German industrial expansion.

Source: Wikipedia – "Bank for International Settlements"
1930
Steel Trust Banking

Stinnes' empire collapses

After Hugo Stinnes' death, his over-leveraged empire, "fattened by depreciated currency," collapsed. German banks had to "come to the rescue with long credits," and the Stinnes heirs were reduced to "no more than coal barons."

Source: Time – "German Business Kaiser"
1932
International US Finance

The Lausanne Conference cancels reparations, but Germany continues paying interest on Dawes and Young bonds until 1939

The Lausanne Conference cancelled reparations, but Germany continued to pay interest on Dawes and Young bonds until 1939. The debt was restructured into bonds generating interest for American banks long after reparations were abandoned.

Source: Wikipedia – "World War I reparations"
1934
US Finance German Industry

35% of German industrial loans come from U.S. banks

By 1934, "35% of the loans were in" default or restructured, but the flow of U.S. capital had already cemented the dominance of integrated conglomerates like Vereinigte Stahlwerke and I.G. Farben, which financed the Nazi war machine.

Source: Time – "German Business Kaiser"
1944
State International

Henry Morgenthau Jr. proposes a plan to eliminate the Ruhr's industrial base

U.S. Treasury Secretary Henry Morgenthau Jr. proposed destroying Germany's arms and civilian industry after the war, including removing or destroying all industrial plants in the Ruhr. Herbert Hoover's investigation concluded the plan was unworkable and would result in up to 25 million German deaths from starvation.

Source: Wikipedia – "Morgenthau Plan"
1945
State International

The Potsdam Agreement attempts to carve up Germany

The Potsdam Agreement divided Germany into occupation zones and established the principle of reparations in industrial equipment and forced labor. It was based on the fiction that Germany could be run as a joint military enterprise while being stripped of industrial capacity—an arrangement that collapsed as the Cold War began.

Source: Fourth International, July–August 1947 – "The Editors: The Marshall Plan"
1947
State International

U.S. policy shifts from destruction to reconstruction

By 1947, the U.S. abandoned the Morgenthau Plan and began planning economic reconstruction of Western Europe as a bulwark against Soviet influence.

Source: Wikipedia – "Morgenthau Plan"
1947
US Finance International State

George C. Marshall announces the Marshall Plan

U.S. Secretary of State George C. Marshall announced the European Recovery Program, offering billions in aid to "buttress the capitalist Western block spearheaded by Washington against the Eastern bloc dominated by the USSR." The plan replaced World Bank loans with direct gifts, which the U.S. concluded would be "more efficient and cost-effective than loans."

Source: CADTM – "The Marshall Plan and the Debt Agreement on German Debt"
1948
US Finance State

The U.S. establishes the KfW to direct German industrial investment

The Anglo-American occupying powers established the Kreditanstalt für Wiederaufbau (KfW), "bankrolled mainly by American Marshall Plan counterpart funds," to "provide investment for German industry." Washington viewed the KfW "as an essential tool in the wider Western response to the challenges of Soviet communism."

Source: Routledge – Reconstruction and Cold War in Germany
1948
US Finance European Integration

Jean Monnet's Wall Street connections shape European integration

Jean Monnet, architect of the Schuman Plan, was a former investment banker with Blair and Co. and founder of Monnet, Murnane and Co. He had "sold bonds on Wall Street" and "served in wartime Washington as a British diplomat." His transatlantic network of bankers and industrialists shaped post-war European order along lines favorable to U.S. financial and corporate interests.

Sources: Time – "Exit the Supranationalist"; gotriple.eu – "Jean Monnet, banker, 1914-1945"
1949
International US Finance State

NATO is created as a tool of U.S. expansion

NATO was founded in 1949. U.S. officials knew the Soviet Union posed no military threat: George Kennan advised the Soviets would "not risk their internal power by military adventures abroad." Nonetheless, "with the pincer grip of the Marshall Plan and NATO, the USA integrated the Western, Northern and Southern European countries into its economic and military expansion."

Source: strategic-culture.su – "NATO: The Founding Lie"
1950
European Integration Cartels

Jean Monnet and Robert Schuman propose the European Coal and Steel Community (ECSC)

Jean Monnet drafted the Schuman Plan, which French Foreign Minister Robert Schuman announced on May 9, 1950. The plan's direct purpose was to solve a material supply problem: French steelmakers needed cheap Ruhr coal to compete. As Time reported, "French steel makers believe that cheap German coal brought to the smelters of Lorraine will enable them to produce steel much more competitively."

German industrialists supported it because it allowed them to avoid dismantlement of their cartels, arguing "the old cartel arrangements were economical and efficient."

Sources: Time – "ECONOMICS: Coal-Steel Pool"; Time – "ECONOMICS: Schuman Plan Deadlock"
1953
US Finance International State

The U.S. orchestrates the cancellation of German debt to secure West Germany as an ally

The London Agreement on German External Debts, led by the United States, cancelled a large portion of Germany's pre-war and post-war debts. "In order to make sure that the economy of West Germany would thrive and thus become a key element of stability in the Atlantic bloc, the creditor allies led by the United States made major concessions to German authorities and corporations—concessions that went beyond debt relief."

Source: CADTM – "The Marshall Plan and the Debt Agreement on German Debt"
1955
US Finance State

The Eisenhower Administration launches the "Nash-Commitment" to build West German armed forces with U.S. funds

Following the Marshall Plan's success, the Eisenhower administration developed the "Nash-Commitment," a plan to build up West German armed forces using American funds, ensuring West Germany's rearmament would be financed by and dependent upon U.S. capital and military contracts.

Source: portal-militaergeschichte.de – "Wirtschaftshilfe"
1963
US Finance State

Boeing acquires a one-third interest in Bölkow GmbH, West Germany's most advanced aerospace research firm

Boeing paid $2 million in cash and $1 million in long-term credits for the stake, a deal that "pries open a position in the growing European aerospace market." This was part of a broader pattern of U.S. defense corporations acquiring direct ownership in European arms firms.

Source: Time – "West Germany: Aerospace Alliance"
1968
RAG State

19 Ruhr industrial companies consolidate their coal assets into Ruhrkohle AG

On November 27, 1968, 19 companies consolidated their coal mining assets into the newly formed Ruhrkohle AG (RAG). The consolidation was forced by the government's threat to withdraw all subsidies. As the company's own history states, "Ruhrkohle AG never paid any dividends to its shareholders," confirming its function was not to generate profit for owners but to manage the "socially compatible" disposal of unprofitable assets with public funds.

Sources: Wikipedia – "RAG AG"; history.evonik.com – "History of Evonik Industries"

Ruhrkohle AG decides to close the Zollverein mines by the end of 1986

Ruhrkohle AG decided to close Zollverein by year-end 1986. The company's stated plan was "den Rückbau und Abriss der Schachtanlage Zollverein XII"—complete demolition and removal of structures for scrap.

The state of North Rhine-Westphalia placed the Zollverein complex under monument protection on December 16, 1986, and purchased it from RAG to preserve it as an industrial monument rather than allowing the owner to scrap it. The mine ceased operations on December 23, 1986.

Ruhrkohle AG shut down the coking plant due to a decline in steel production and resulting fall in coke demand. Plans to sell it to China fell through.

On March 17, 1999, Thyssen AG and Krupp merged into ThyssenKrupp AG, aiming "to solve the cost and production problems facing the iron and steel industry caused by excess capacities."

UNESCO inscribes Zollverein as a World Heritage Site

On December 14, 2001, the UNESCO World Heritage Committee inscribed the Zollverein Coal Mine Industrial Complex on the World Heritage List.

2007
RAG Krupp Thyssen State Banking

Werner Müller creates the RAG-Stiftung, allowing E.ON, RWE, and ThyssenKrupp to offload perpetual mining liabilities for one euro per share

Werner Müller, former Bundeswirtschaftsminister and then CEO of RAG, founded the RAG-Stiftung on June 26, 2007. He structured a deal whereby the existing RAG shareholders—E.ON AG, RWE AG, ThyssenKrupp AG, and Société Nouvelle Sidéchar SARL—sold their shares to the new foundation for a symbolic one euro each.

In exchange, these corporations permanently shed all liability for perpetual mining damages: groundwater pumping, subsidence repair, and water purification. Müller simultaneously spun off RAG's profitable chemicals division into Evonik Industries AG, with the RAG-Stiftung holding a majority stake (initially 64.3%). Dividends from Evonik now flow to the foundation to pay the perpetual costs, ensuring the former mine owners escape financial responsibility while the public budget remains untouched.

Sources: Wikipedia – "RAG-Stiftung"; Wikipedia – "Werner Müller"; rag-stiftung.de – "RAG-Stiftung Kapitalanlagen"

Glossary

Zollverein (German: "Customs Union")
A coalition of German states formed in 1834 to manage tariffs and economic policies within their territories. It eliminated internal customs barriers while maintaining external tariffs, creating a unified market favoring member-state industrialists. Franz Haniel named his mine after this union.
Gewerkschaft (German: "Mining Corporation")
A specific legal form of business organization used in German mining. Unlike a joint-stock company, shares (Kuxe) in a Gewerkschaft could be held in a closed circle and were not publicly traded, allowing families like the Haniels to retain complete control over profits and operations.
Thaler (also Taler)
A silver coin and unit of currency used across the German states before the introduction of the Mark in 1871. One thaler was worth approximately 3 marks. The word "dollar" derives from "thaler."
Cartel (German: Kartell)
A formal agreement among competing firms to fix prices, divide markets, or limit production. Unlike in common law countries where such agreements were generally unenforceable, the German Reichsgericht ruled in 1897 that cartel contracts were legally binding, making Germany uniquely hospitable to industrial monopolies.
Langnam-Verein
Short for "Verein zur Wahrung der gemeinsamen wirtschaftlichen Interessen in Rheinland und Westfalen." Founded 1871 to lobby for protective tariffs and cartel-friendly legislation for Rhenish-Westphalian heavy industry.
Centralverband Deutscher Industrieller
The Central Association of German Industrialists. A national-level lobby group founded in 1876, it represented heavy industry interests (steel, coal, iron) at the Reichstag level and successfully pushed for protectionist trade policy and legal tolerance of cartels.
RWKS (Rheinisch-Westfälisches Kohlen-Syndikat)
The Rhenish-Westphalian Coal Syndicate, founded 1893. A cartel that gave its members collective control over coal output and pricing in the Ruhr. At its peak it controlled over 90% of Ruhr coal production.
Villa Hügel
The 269-room private residence of the Krupp family in Essen, completed in 1873. It served as both a home and a venue for political meetings, including the first gathering of the Ruhrlade in 1928. Today it is a museum and cultural center.
Ruhrlade
A secret organization of twelve leading Ruhr industrialists, founded in 1928 at Villa Hügel. Its purpose was to coordinate political donations and lobby strategy across the Weimar Republic. Members included the heads of major steel, coal, and mining companies.
Dawes Plan (1924)
An American-designed restructuring of Germany's reparations obligations, largely organized by J.P. Morgan. It placed Germany in financial "receivership," provided an 800-million gold mark loan, and created a circular flow of capital: U.S. loans → German reparations payments → Allied war debt repayments to U.S. banks. Replaced by the Young Plan in 1929.
BIS (Bank for International Settlements)
An international financial institution established in 1930 under the Young Plan to administer German reparation payments. Often called the "central bank for central banks," it was controlled by the same banker network that had designed the Dawes and Young Plans.
ECSC (European Coal and Steel Community)
The first supranational European institution, proposed in 1950 via the Schuman Plan. It placed French and German coal and steel production under a common authority. Often cited as the precursor to the European Union; its founding was also driven by French industrialists' desire for cheap Ruhr coal and German industrialists' desire to preserve cartel structures.
RAG (Ruhrkohle AG)
A holding company formed in 1968 when 19 Ruhr companies consolidated their coal assets under government pressure. RAG never paid dividends; its function was to manage the orderly decline of coal mining with public subsidies. In 2007 it was restructured into the RAG-Stiftung foundation.
Stiftung (German: "Foundation")
A legal entity under German law that holds assets in perpetuity for a defined purpose. The RAG-Stiftung was created specifically to absorb the perpetual environmental liabilities of Ruhr coal mining, funded by dividends from the profitable Evonik Industries spin-off.
Vereinigte Stahlwerke (German Steel Trust)
A steel conglomerate created in 1926 by the New York investment bank Dillon, Read & Co. using Dawes Plan capital. It merged the Thyssen, Phoenix, Rheinstal, and Deutsch-Luxemburg steel works, controlling more than 75% of Germany's ore reserves and employing 200,000 workers.
KfW (Kreditanstalt für Wiederaufbau)
"Reconstruction Credit Institute." Established in 1948 by Anglo-American occupying powers and bankrolled by Marshall Plan funds to channel investment into German industry. Still exists today as Germany's state development bank.
